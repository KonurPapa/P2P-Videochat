Gemini said

To implement this, you’ll be using NIP-17 (Private Direct Messages) as your signaling transport. NIP-17 is superior to the older NIP-04 because it uses "Gift Wraps" (NIP-59) to hide metadata—even the relay won’t know who is calling whom.
WebRTC Signaling via Nostr

In a standard WebRTC setup, you'd use a WebSocket server. Here, the Nostr Relay becomes that server. The "Signaling" involves exchanging two things: SDP (Session Description Protocol) and ICE Candidates (network routing info).
The Sequence Flow

    Initiation: Alice creates a WebRTC "Offer" (SDP).

    Signaling (Step 1): Alice wraps this SDP in a NIP-17 Gift Wrap and publishes it to a relay.

    Discovery: Bob, who is "listening" for Gift Wraps addressed to his public key, receives and unwraps the event.

    Acceptance: Bob creates a WebRTC "Answer" (SDP), wraps it, and sends it back via the relay.

    Direct Connection: Once both have the SDPs, they exchange ICE Candidates (still via Nostr) to find a direct network path.

    P2P Stream: The video/audio data flows directly between Alice and Bob. Nostr is no longer involved.

Key Technical Considerations

    Ephemeral Events: Use Kind 14 (standard NIP-17) or a custom Kind range (20000-29999). These are ephemeral, meaning relays are instructed not to store them long-term. This keeps your "signaling" clean and reduces relay bloat.

    TURN Servers: As mentioned before, if Alice and Bob are both behind symmetric NATs (common in corporate offices), the P2P connection will fail. You'll need to offer a TURN server. To keep it "dev-focused," you could let users input their own TURN credentials in the settings.

    Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS): Since NIP-17 uses NIP-44 for encryption, you get much better security than the legacy NIP-04. However, for a video messenger, you might eventually look into NIP-104 (an experimental proposal for OLM/Signal-style ratcheting) if you want state-of-the-art E2EE.

Developer Marketing: "The Transparency Feature"

Holesail is an excellent choice for this, especially given your focus on developers and "sovereign" tech. It actually solves the NAT Traversal (firewall) problem in a more decentralized way than standard WebRTC.

Here is the breakdown of how Holesail compares to Nostr and why you might actually want to use both.
1. Holesail vs. WebRTC + Nostr

While WebRTC usually requires a TURN server (a centralized relay) to bypass strict firewalls, Holesail uses the Hypercore Protocol (specifically HyperDHT).

    How it works: Holesail performs "UDP Holepunching" using a Distributed Hash Table (DHT). Instead of a central server helping you find your friend, the entire network of Holesail nodes acts as the "facilitator."

    The Benefit: It is "truly P2P." There is no single point of failure or surveillance. If two peers can't connect directly, the DHT nodes coordinate the punch.

    Security: Holesail uses the Noise Protocol (the same one used by WireGuard and WhatsApp) and libsodium for encryption. It is exceptionally secure and designed for "zero-knowledge" connectivity.

2. Can you use both?

You don't have to choose! In fact, using them together creates a powerhouse architecture:

    Nostr as the "Identity & Signaling" Layer: Use Nostr to find your friends, share your public keys, and send the initial "Hey, I'm online, connect to me" message.

    Holesail as the "Data Tunnel" Layer: Once Alice and Bob find each other on Nostr, they use Holesail to create a secure, encrypted tunnel that punches through their firewalls. You then stream the video data through this tunnel.

3. Comparison of Protocols
Feature	Nostr	Holesail	WebRTC
Primary Use	Messaging & Discovery	P2P Tunneling (TCP/UDP)	Real-time Video/Audio
Firewall Bypass	N/A (Relay-based)	HyperDHT Holepunching	STUN/TURN Servers
Metadata	Visible to Relays (unless NIP-17)	Highly Obfuscated	Visible to TURN provider
Setup Complexity	Low	Medium (needs Node.js/CLI)	High (ICE/SDP handshakes)
